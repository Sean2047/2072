## 7.6 Structural Conditions for the End of the Turbulent Period

### 7.6.1 Why Neither Great Power Wanted the Other to Descend into Chaos

The deep structure of the Turbulent Period generated a stabilizing mechanism that appears counterintuitive: A1 and A2, even as they competed fiercely, retained a common interest in the preservation of each other's basic order. This was not ideological convergence; it was an objective constraint built into the structure of their interests.

A war between the great powers during the Turbulent Period carried costs beyond what either side could absorb. The realization of the AI and fusion dividends depended on the continued functioning of global supply chains, technological diffusion, and financial stability—conditions that a hot war would have destroyed outright. For A1, war would have severed the international capital flows and engineering collaboration on which the commercialization of fusion depended; for A2, it would have cut the import channels and export markets on which its technological upgrading relied. Neither side could have completed its own passage through the Turbulent Period under conditions of war.

Nor was the other's collapse any more desirable. A large-scale political breakdown in A2 would have produced hundreds of millions of refugees, a loss of control over nuclear weapons, and the rupture of global supply chains—consequences whose impact on the A1 system would have been no less severe than that of a war itself. An institutional collapse of A1 would have deprived A2 of its most important source of technology and its largest export markets, while plunging the global financial system into a disorder that A2 could not have sustained alone. This was a vulnerability of mutual dependence: two systems each too strong to be overwhelmed by the other, and too deeply intertwined to remain insulated from the other's ruin.

This structure kept great-power competition within an implicit boundary throughout the Turbulent Period: intense, but never crossing the threshold beyond which the other's systemic collapse would be triggered. The Taiwan Strait conflict (see 7.5.4) was the concrete historical moment at which that boundary was tested—and confirmed.

### 7.6.2 The Core Indicator of the End: The Maturation of BSC

The central tension of the Turbulent Period was that AI-driven employment displacement outran the capacity of existing institutions to supply an alternative guarantee of subsistence. That tension did not dissolve of its own accord with the passage of time. Only when a new mechanism of provision reached maturity—in both coverage and fiscal sustainability—did the underlying driver of the Turbulent Period truly disappear.

The maturation of Basic Support Credits (BSC) is the single most important indicator of the Turbulent Period's end. That maturation comprised three layers: *completeness of coverage* (the great majority of those hit by employment displacement fell within the system's protection); *fiscal sustainability* (the system's financing rested not on the expansion of debt but on stable taxation of the AI productivity dividend); and *political stability* (the mechanism had survived at least one full political cycle, ceasing to be a policy that might be overturned at any moment and becoming an institutional fact broadly accepted by society).

The fiscal foundation of BSC rested on a precondition: only after the commercialization of fusion (around 2044) did the productivity surge driven by cheap energy make the tax base of the AI dividend large enough to support redistribution on the necessary scale. The maturation of BSC therefore necessarily came later than fusion's commercialization—fusion provided the material foundation; BSC completed the institutional transformation. The full causal chain ran: *fusion commercialization → systematic decline in energy costs → productivity surge driven by AI plus cheap energy → expansion of the tax base → fiscal viability of BSC → employment no longer the sole guarantee of subsistence → dissolution of the Turbulent Period's central tension.*

Within the A1 system the window for BSC maturation fell roughly in 2052–2058, with variation across national paths (earliest in the Nordic countries, latest in the democracies of East Asia). The A2 system had no BSC in name, but its state rationing economy achieved a functionally similar coverage on a broadly similar schedule.

The end of the Turbulent Period did not mean that the problem had been solved. It meant that the problem had changed in kind: from "this system may collapse" to "this system is running—but at what cost?" The latter is a question a society can carry in steady state for a long time; it is not the kind of question that forces a system to break.
